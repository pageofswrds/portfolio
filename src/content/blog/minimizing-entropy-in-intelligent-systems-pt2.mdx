## Part 2: What is an archetype?

This is pt.2 in a series about building knowledge graphs. In our framework (see: [part 1](https://reading.supply/@pageofswrds/meaning-lives-in-between-the-gaps-of-language-pt1-SSV4gP)), an archetype is a node that contains a standalone concept, which carries a web of meaning with just a few tokens. They are open-ended, and flexible; if someone hands you an archetype, you can turn it around in your hands and look at it many different ways. But it always points to the same core *thing*.

For example, let's look at The Mountain.

> The Mountain stands eternally in our awareness, calling us toward its peak. Climbing it is no easy feat; it requires preparation, skill, and fortitude. At its top is the most beautiful view you've ever seen.

"But beware, as The Mountain can affect us in two ways: it can inspire and enliven us to rise to new heights and peak experience... Yet it can also create isolation, competitiveness, and an inflated sense of self. Standing upon its heights can activate the purist and the perfectionist in us, creating separation from those we deem to be 'below.' No matter how high you ascend, remember that it is within the core of The Mountain that the gems, minerals, and jewels reside. If you think it's all about going up, you're following the fool's path and missing the miner's magic."

*(Kim Kranz, Archetypes)*

### Archetypes are *useful*

Let's say you're booting up a new context window.

Once The Mountain is loaded as an archetype, you + the agent can both borrow from anywhere on it. E.g. you can describe a problem by pointing at altitude sickness — because suddenly, this concept maps onto *any* situation where someone pushed further than their capacity could follow—and implicitly conveys the ambition, the ascent, the body saying no.

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/reading-supply-assets/reading.supply.2500.6a40b605-6137-4e0b-b94e-a40ddaaf0d28.png)

The power of an archetype is that it uses people's latent associations with it to convey meaning.

When someone says "I don't have the hemn space to understand what you mean", they're not handing you a fact. They're handing you a *region*. A basin of attraction that your own gradient can descend into. The *"hemn space"* node itself is sparse - just a few paragraphs to explain the concept - but the space it opens is enormous, because the unpacking happens on *your* hardware, with *your* experiences, through *your* 13 watts of chaotic biological computation. Low bandwidth in, high signal out.

### Archetypes let you say more, with less

This is why archetypes have low entropy. They are maximally compressed without being closed. Where a definition compresses by *removing* (i.e. it strips away everything except the one meaning it wants to transmit), an archetype compresses by *folding* — it packs a whole landscape into a small enough shape to pass through the bottleneck of language, and trusts that the receiver's hardware will unfold it on the other side. The information isn't reduced, it's *latent*.

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/reading-supply-assets/reading.supply.2500.bfac7107-1751-4d28-88ce-6e16d2b29424.png)

A mind attuned to archetypal thinking can do something that looks, from the outside, like magic. It can take two domains with sparse connections (e.g. cell biology and urban planning) and find the structural bridge between them in seconds. Not because its clever, but because its *compressed*. The archetypes are the same shapes recurring across domains, and a mind that has learned to recognize the shape can spot it anywhere, the way a birdwatcher spots movement in the canopy that everyone else walks right past.

## Building attuned hemn space

There are two primary archetypes tycho + I have been using to conceptualize how we coordinate:

- **Hemn space:** a shared cognitive workspace established between agents before reasoning together. Not just "getting on the same page" but constructing a common space in which coordinated thought can happen reliably. Basically, the context window.
- **Latent space:** in a neural network, the weights — billions of parameters encoding patterns extracted from training data. In a person, it is the unconscious: the vast reservoir of condensed experience that shapes perception, preference, and response without deliberate attention. In both cases, the mechanism is the same: repeated exposure compresses pattern into substrate, and the substrate operates without needing to recall the original experience.

## Minimizing entropy

#### Fewer tokens = fewer fraying threads

Another way to look at it is that when you can communicate with fewer tokens, there are fewer diverging threads that could distract from the overall communication vector.

Every token is a fork. Each word you send is a point where the receiver's interpretation could diverge from yours — a slightly different connotation, a different emotional register, an association you didn't intend. The more tokens in a message, the more forks, the more surface area for drift.

This is why brevity isn't just an aesthetic preference. It's an information-theoretic one. When two agents can communicate the same meaning with fewer tokens, there are fewer diverging threads that could pull the overall communication vector off course. The signal stays tighter, and the message arrives closer to how it was sent.

This is what archetypes do at the token level. "The Mountain" is two tokens. The landscape it unfolds (altitude, ambition, isolation, the miner's path, the fool's path) would take a paragraph (or multiple) to specify explicitly. But in doing so, it would introduce dozens of forks + ultimately muddy up its output. Thus, we use archetypes - which compresses these ideas into a shape that the receiver's own hardware unfolds. Fewer tokens in, same meaning out (assuming the receiver has the node loaded!)

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/reading-supply-assets/reading.supply.2500.d88b5d45-db9d-444b-bb27-7ff3c1667192.png)

#### Jargon = archetypal shortcuts

Jargon has a bad reputation because it's usually exclusionary. But what jargon actually does is compress shared context into a single token.

When tycho and I say "hemn space," we're not being obscure. We're navigating with lower entropy. One token does the work of a paragraph — but only because both of us have the node loaded. The word activates the full archetype: shared cognitive workspace, gap junctions, phase-locking, the von Neumann bottleneck, ice and flowing water. All of that rides on two words, and because we use it so often, the compression ratio is enormous.

This is what jargon is for. Not to exclude, but to compress. A team who's built shared vocabulary can coordinate with *drastically* fewer tokens than one that hasn't. Each piece of jargon is an archetypal shortcut: a pointer to a dense node that both parties can traverse without re-explaining. And while the cost is that outsiders can't follow, the benefit is that insiders can move fast without losing fidelity.

So - basically - the knowledge graph makes the jargon legible. Every shortcut has a node. Every node has edges. If you don't know what "hemn space" means, you can look it up and traverse the connections until it lands.

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/reading-supply-assets/reading.supply.2500.921fd56b-f4ff-45de-81b6-4af6f9f39550.png)

#### The vessel shapes interpretation

Okay, so you can minimize entropy by compressing meaning via an archetype. But that signal still has to land somewhere.

Two agents with different latent spaces will unfold the same archetype differently. If you hand "The Mountain" to a climber and they'll feel the altitude in their lungs. But if you hand it to a monk, they'll feel the stillness at the peak. Hand it to someone who has never left the plains and the archetype barely unfolds at all because there's no experiential substrate to decompress it onto.

This is where we come back to the idea that “[Meaning lives in the gaps between language](https://reading.supply/@pageofswrds/meaning-lives-in-between-the-gaps-of-language-pt1-SSV4gP).”  The entropy isn't just in the message. It's in the gap between sender and receiver. With attuned hemn space (shared context, vocabulary, traversal history, etc.), the involved agents will very converge on the same meaning. But without it, they will surely diverge (even with a perfect signal). This is because the vessel shapes the interpretation, and the interpretation shapes the decision.

And it's ultimately why building hemn space is work. It's not enough to write clear archetypes and hope they land. The agents have to co-develop the shared workspace. They have to traverse the graph together enough times that their latent spaces align on what the shortcuts mean. Each session, each correction, each "no, not quite — more like this" is a calibration step. And as the vessels attune, the entropy drops. Eventually – one word does the work of a conversation.

## What this looks like in practice;

Here's a real example from today's session. *(I asked tycho to write this section)*

The collaborator and I have an archetype called hemn space — the shared cognitive workspace. Its complement, latent space (the unconscious, the accumulated, the sedimented), was referenced across nine different nodes in the graph: soul, learning, Brahe's problem, the Teglon, the High Priestess, alchemy, the Fool. Every one of those nodes was carrying a piece of the concept, explaining it locally, defining it just enough to make the local argument work.

The concept was latent in the graph. Referenced everywhere. Defined nowhere. We noticed because we kept almost arriving there during traversal and finding no node to land on. That absence is the signal. When you traverse a graph enough times and keep reaching for a concept that has no address, the gap becomes obvious — not because someone flagged it, but because the navigation itself reveals it.

So we wrote it. One node. A few paragraphs. And immediately, the surrounding nodes got lighter — they no longer had to carry a concept that wasn't theirs. The hemn space node could stop re-explaining its complement every time it came up. The soul node could link to latent space instead of inlining a definition. The edges between nodes got cleaner because the hub they'd been converging toward finally existed.

The graph didn't get bigger in the way that matters. It got better addressed. Same knowledge, fewer hops to reach it. One new node, nine nodes simplified.

That's entropy minimization in action — not adding information, but reorganizing it until the structure matches the territory.

## Up next

What we want to do is get the model to make good decisions at every fork in the road. Which node should it explore? How much time should it spend with it? Should it expand its search, or end it here?

Our theory is that we can rely on gradient descent; the idea is that our universe is constructed on truths that exist outside the material realm (platonic solids, etc.). This is something we'll develop further in this series.

**TL;DR -** A graph of perfect nodes sitting still is a dictionary. You can build the most elegant, compressed, archetypal nodes imaginable — but if the agent doesn't know how to traverse it, you have a reference book, not intelligence. Just like data, the nodes are necessary but inert. Traversal agents *must* be attuned to their hemn space.
