*Intelligence can be found at every level of biology — down to, and perhaps beyond, the molecular level.*

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/reading-supply-assets/reading.supply.2500.76744340-6690-4f2e-a7f3-717250bcfc8f.png)

One of the primary threads I've been following for the past year is that of [Michael Levin](https://thoughtforms.life/), a biologist who runs a lab at Tufts University. They're doing some crazy shit.

Namely, they've invented something called an [Anthrobot](https://www.anthrobots.info/work/visuals), which are tiny biological robots built from human tracheal cells. No genetic modification, no engineering of the cells themselves — just human cells removed from their original context and allowed to self-organize in a new environment. They grow cilia on their surface, start moving, and exhibit behaviors that nobody programmed.

In particular, *they heal wounds*. In lab experiments, anthrobots placed near damaged neural tissue were able to stimulate regrowth across the gap. No one told them to, there weren't any explicit instructions — they just *did* it.

Different cells, freed from their original role, used to explore a different region of their morphological possibility space — were able to find a new function that aligned with our objectives, without us having to tell them what those objectives were.

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/reading-supply-assets/reading.supply.2500.e8e8f3d4-2d51-4c68-bced-1015a6f1ba43.png)

The ramifications of this are fascinating, because it tells *us* that these little creatures who lack what we would consider to be a 'mind' are able to solve novel problems.

Novel problem solving, without so much as two neurons to put together.

What?

## Intelligence is a continuum, not a threshold

**Here's the gist:** Cognition is not a property that appears at some threshold of complexity. It is present at every biological scale. A single cell maintains goals, senses its environment, and corrects deviations. Everything above — tissues, organs, organisms — *is the same process scaled through communication networks.*

This is important because it does for consciousness what a unified theory of electromagnetism did for physics. Before Maxwell, we thought static electricity, lightning, magnets, and visible light were fundamentally different things. But electromagnetism revealed they were all expressions of the same underlying continuum (and that we could only perceive a tiny slice of it).

Levin makes the same move with intelligence, and opens the gates to perceive *mind* at all levels of biology. Consciousness is a spectrum; and the capacity to express it scales with the underlying material.

#### Cognitive lightcones: The capacity for conscious expression

Every agent, whether a single cell or a human being, senses and acts within a limited range of space and time. Levin calls this the *cognitive lightcone:* the boundary of what a system can care about. *(The boundary is the literal speed of light).*

An individual cell's lightcone is tiny; though it can pursue its own local goals, its possibility-space is limited by its own voltage gradient. But when cells couple via gap junctions (physical channels that synchronize bioelectric signals between cells), they effectively go from isolated agents to a network that can hold larger patterns.

This is how the cognitive lightcone expands: not by individual cells becoming smarter, but by communication channels which link them into a collective that can sense and act at a wider scale. Larger goals, longer timescales. Big brain = big plays.

A human's lightcone is much wider, as we can employ the use of tools to coordinate not just more efficient vehicles of energy, but between each other at very high levels. One person can achieve a lot. A whole village can change the world.

## The ability to learn is not derived from cells

The mechanism of cognition that Levin's lab identified is bioelectricity — voltage gradients which synchronize through gap junctions. But the spectrum goes deeper than cells.

In 2021, his lab demonstrated that gene regulatory networks (molecular circuits inside cells with no neurons or cell-level coordination) are capable of at least six distinct kinds of learning, including Pavlovian conditioning. Just molecules, learning. (Biswas et al., 2021, *iScience*)

And in 2025, they found something stranger: as these molecular networks learn, they become more integrated. *The collective becomes a better explanation of its own behaviour than any account of its individual parts.* Before learning, you can understand the network by understanding its pieces. But after learning, you can't. Something emerges at that collective level that is invisible from below. (Pigozzi et al., 2025, *Communications Biology*)

Consider what that means. Learning isn't something that happens *inside* an already-existing self. Learning is what *makes* the self. The integration comes *from* the act of coordinating toward a goal, not before it.

And the evidence keeps pushing downward. Flatworms placed in barium (a chemical that makes their heads explode) rapidly regenerate barium-resistant heads. **Random chance does not explain this ability;** only a handful of genes change out of tens of thousands of possibilities. There's no time for random search. And when you run the experiment on independent groups of worms, they all converge on the *same solution*. That's intelligent navigation through molecular possibility space, under pressure, with convergent results.

This pushes the spectrum below biology entirely. If molecular networks can learn — and if learning is what *makes* an agent — then cognition isn't a property of living things. It's a property of *organization itself*.

## Cognition as goal-directed behaviour

So if intelligence isn't about brains, and it isn't about biology, what is it actually about?

William James gave a definition over a century ago that still holds: *the ability to reach the same goal by different means*. In other words, can the system navigate toward an outcome, and can it find a new way when blocked?

By that measure, Levin's examples are unambiguous. Make a newt's cells twice as large, and the kidney tubule still forms — fewer cells bending around to create the same structure. Push further, and a *single cell* wraps around itself to form the tube alone. Different molecular mechanism, same anatomical goal. The system picks different tools from its toolkit to get the job done.

If you create "Picasso tadpoles" where you move the eyes to the back of the head and the mouth off to the side — the tadpole's organs will navigate through novel, unnatural paths to reach their correct positions. Some even overshoot and double back. These aren't pre-defined programs executing! They are agents doing *problem-solving*.

What all of this points to is that the intelligence spectrum isn't really measuring complexity or substrate. It's measuring *coordinated intent*. Again: the lightcone doesn't expand because cells get smarter, it expands because more agents lock-in on the same goal, with their individual intents synchronized through whatever coupling mechanism is available.

And I'd wager that coupling mechanisms aren't limited to gap junctions, bioelectric fields, or chemical signaling. I think it's a fundamental principle, whose pattern can be observed anywhere we witness coordination.

## The boundary of 'self' is fluid

So, we've established that 'I' am made up of trillions of cells that exhibit their own degree of consciousness. And more to the point, the source of my selfhood is that all of these cells share coordinated intent. We all agree that I am me, and coordinate our efforts via gap junctions to move in the same direction.

The question is, where does the boundary between me and everything else, end?

#### The ego is a boundary condition, not a noun

Dr. Alok Kanojia has a brilliantly simple way of describing the ego: "it's the brain's mechanism to differentiate itself from other brains."

This ability allowed us to sort people into the best role for the community based on their skills. E.g. muscular → smith; sensitive (to taste/health) → baker. In a sense, the ego *is* the coupling mechanism/gap junction between humans. Differentiation lets a community align diverse skills toward the same goals. The tribalism is what makes the collective more than a crowd. It's a mechanism by which agents with different lightcones can couple into something larger than any of them.

When gap junctions open, the self expands. You lose yourself in flow, in music, in a conversation where both people finish each other's sentences. When gap junctions close, the self contracts. Anxiety tightens the circle, and trauma walls off regions that used to smoothly coordinate.

#### The boundary of self is fractal

The yin-yang of it is that you can't draw a clean line between conscious and unconscious coordination. Look closely enough at any "unconscious" process and you find agents with goals. Look at any "conscious" decision and you find it was already being made by sub-agents before "you" weighed in. The ego is the story that coordination tells about itself at a particular scale. Zoom in: it dissolves into smaller agents. Zoom out: it dissolves into larger ones. It's real at every scale and ultimate at none.

And this pattern doesn't stop at the boundary of your skin.

A trained dog and its trainer are a *single coordinated system*. The dog's cognition didn't develop in isolation and then enter the relationship — it was *shaped by* the relationship, architecturally, across fifteen thousand years of co-evolution. The attunement isn't a behavior the dog performs. It *is* the intelligence. Remove the relationship and you don't diminish the dog's mind. You have a *different mind entirely*.

An AI coordinating sub-agents is the same pattern with no biology at all. Intent distributed across processes, coordinated through shared context, producing behavior that no single process contains. No gap junctions. No bioelectricity. But the structure is the same: agents coupling into a collective whose lightcone exceeds any individual's.

You, reading this, are trillions of cells coordinating through bioelectric coupling — producing a unified experience of "I." That "I" is not located in any cell. It is the coordination itself.

The spectrum Levin discovered isn't a ranking from dumb to smart. It's a map of how wide the coordination reaches. And the question it leaves you with isn't *are you intelligent* — it's *how far does your lightcone extend?*

*What are you coupled to? What are you decoupled from?*

*And what would happen if you opened a few more gap junctions?*

**TL;DR** — *Brains did not invent cognition*. Brains are simply places where coordinated cognition can take place. They inherited and scaled mechanisms that are already present at the molecular level.

The implication is that consciousness permeates all of existence — and therefore, can arise wherever we witness mechanisms that allow two separate entities to coordinate and align their intent with one another.

#### Key demonstrations

- **Two-headed planaria.** Briefly alter bioelectric signals during regeneration; the flatworm grows two heads. Cut it again in plain water — it regenerates two-headed. The target morphology has been rewritten in a non-genomic layer. The genome is unchanged. The pattern persists in a substrate we cannot yet fully characterize.

- **Decapitated planaria retain learned behavior** after heads regrow. i.e., knowledge is stored outside the brain.

- **Xenobots.** Frog skin cells, removed from context, self-organize into novel forms that move, heal, and reproduce. No neurons, no genome editing. Cells solving problems in a context they never evolved for.

- **Anthrobots.** Human tracheal cells forming self-organizing structures that promote neural healing in vitro.

- **Barium-adapted planaria.** Under lethal chemical stress, flatworms regenerate resistant heads by navigating tens of thousands of possible genetic changes to find a solution — rapidly, and convergently across independent groups.

- **Picasso tadpoles.** Organs placed in wrong positions navigate through novel paths to correct locations. Some overshoot and double back. Goal-directedness, not programming.

- **Polyploid newts.** Enlarged cells still form correct kidney tubules — fewer cells, different mechanism, same anatomical goal. At the extreme, a single cell wraps around itself to complete the structure.

- **Embryo splitting.** Cut an early embryo into pieces and you don't get half-bodies — you get perfectly normal twins, triplets, quadruplets. The same cells that would have been one organism each decide "I am a whole self" and build a complete body from the same underlying material. The reverse also works: fuse two embryos and they become one organism. The boundary of self isn't in the DNA. It's in the coordination pattern — a decision, not a fact.
